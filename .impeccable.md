## Design Context

### Users
Corporate decision-makers — VP of Operations, Facilities Directors, Real Estate leads — evaluating project management firms for commercial construction, office fit-outs, retail builds, and institutional renovations. They browse during business hours on desktops, often comparing against 2–3 other firms. They need to feel confident this is a serious, experienced outfit that won't fumble a multi-million dollar project. Secondary audience: institutional and government procurement officers who need evidence of track record and capability.

### Brand Personality
**Seasoned, meticulous, and straight-talking.** This is a family-run firm (the Nissenbaums) with nearly 30 years in the field — not a faceless corporation, but not scrappy either. They've managed projects for Nike, BCG, the Government of Canada, and the CBC. The personality is "the most experienced person in the room who doesn't need to prove it with flash — their work speaks for itself."

### Aesthetic Direction
- **Tone**: Architectural editorial — think the confidence of a well-designed building portfolio, not a tech landing page. Clean, structured, with moments of visual weight that convey permanence and precision.
- **Theme**: Hybrid — dark header/hero that transitions into light content areas. Clients browse in office settings during business hours.
- **Anti-references**: Generic construction sites (orange hard hats, yellow caution tape palettes). SaaS landing pages (floating UI mockups, purple gradients). Cookie-cutter v0/template sites with identical card grids.
- **References**: Architecture firm portfolios. The quiet authority of a well-set monograph. Firms like Foster + Partners or Perkins&Will — the website equivalent of a confident handshake.

### Design Principles
1. **Let the work lead** — Project photography and client names carry more weight than any graphic treatment. Design around showcasing real outcomes.
2. **Earned authority** — 30 years of experience and a roster of national brands should be felt, not shouted. Restraint signals confidence.
3. **Structured clarity** — Construction management is about organization and foresight. The site should reflect that: clear hierarchy, logical flow, no visual clutter.
4. **Personal touch** — This is a family business. The human element (team, direct contact, personal bios) should feel warm and accessible without being folksy.
5. **Built to last** — The aesthetic should feel timeless, not trendy. No design fads that will date within 18 months.
